Saba Saba: Raila Odinga, Other Kenyan Politicians' Roles in Fight for Multiparty Democracy

  • Kenya marks Saba Saba, the day Kenyans took to the streets to protest against one-party State rule
  • The late president Daniel Toroitich Arap Moi had ruled Kenya with an iron fist through KANU and young leaders expressed the desire to change how democracy would work
  • Raila Odinga's role in planning the demonstrations led to his arrest and detention by Moi's government

On July 7, 1990, Kenyans marched to the streets to fight for a multi-party democracy loosely translated in Kiswahili as ‘saba saba’ means every seventh day of the seventh month of the year.

The young patriots during that time organised a seismic challenge that led to demonstrations to repeal section 2A of Kenya's old constitution to allow for multiparty democracy.

This significant event is popularly referred to as Kenya's second liberation, only coming after the nation freed itself from colonial rule about 28 years earlier.

The demonstrations were to be held at Kamukunji Grounds in Kenya's capital city of Nairobi.

The grand march was a reaction by Kenyans and a section of political elites who had lived under the dictatorship of Kenya’s second president the late Daniel Toroitich Arap Moi with his one-party rule.

The man who served as Kenya's founding president Jomo Kenyatta's vice president before the latter's demise, presided over the country through Kenya African National Union(KANU).

Moi’s government was uncomfortable with the influence the young turks had in the country and would do all it could to stamp its authority against the young revolutionary leaders craving for change.

The man from Baringo county began using State machinery to target the revolutionaries.

Proponents of the much looked-forward to protest like Charles Rubia, Raila Odinga and Kenneth Matiba were arrested and detained before the actual day of protest but this did not stop an idea whose time had undoubtedly come.

Faces Behind the Revolution

Charles Rubia was Starehe constituency MP before he was put in detention in 1990 and released a year later.

Kenneth Matiba, then, an ardent activist for democratic reforms, was MP for Mbiri in Kirinyaga County between 1979 to 1990.

Raila Odinga was walking in the footsteps of his father Jaramongi Oginga Odinga who was a crusader of democracy. He was put in detention in 1990 and released in 1991.

There was enough anger in the public that the rally at Kamukunji grounds went on despite the intimidation and arrests of the demonstration’s leaders.

The citizens who went to hold the event at Kamukunji grounds were met by uniformed police officers who clobbered and lobbed tear gas canisters at them.

It was reported at least 21 people died and thousands were injured in the confrontation between police officers and protesting citizens.

Top lawyers Paul Muite, Gitobu Imanyara, and James Orengo among others led the demonstrations after the arrest of the frontrunners, but they were also arrested at Kamukunji Grounds.

The friction between protesters and Moi's administration ushered in a contentious stage for a second liberation conversation that birthed the multi-party democracy.

More than 30 years later, the July 7, event has never been recognised as a national holiday to honour the departed and living heroes who led the dangerous journey to freedom.

However, the day is commemorated by Kenyans as a day to speak out and act against an oppressive regime.

Human rights defenders use this day to lobby for the end of police brutality unleashed on protestors.

Moi Succumbs to Mounting Pressure

In December 1991, KANU held a special conference introducing a multi-party political system the young liberators had fought for.

More than 30 years later, Kenya has embraced the multi-party system, and politicians have introduced uncountable parties.

Lately, ahead of each General Election, parties with the same interests have agreed to fold and form a behemoth that their preferred presidential candidate uses to contest for the top seat.

The birth of coalitions or alliances, as they are popularly known, can not be written about without the first Saba Saba liberation demonstration.

Truly, those who paid the ultimate price and those who got injured would be pleased to see steps made, but their joy would well up if the worrying corruption rates in government were eliminated.

Raila Odinga announces civil disobedience

On Tuesday, July 4, Raila announced a nationwide civil disobedience to revolt against what he terms "William Ruto's punitive taxes".

"Our sole weapon is our sovereign power to protest, boycott, and engage in civil disobedience. We exercise this power starting on Friday, July 7th," Raila Odinga said.

The Azimio la Umoja One Kenya Coalition Party leader said the law allowed them to protest and picket if all other avenues fail.
