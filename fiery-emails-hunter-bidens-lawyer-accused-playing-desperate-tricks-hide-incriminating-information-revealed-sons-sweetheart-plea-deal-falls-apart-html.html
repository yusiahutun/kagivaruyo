EXCLUSIVE: Fiery emails from Hunter Biden's lawyer after being accused of playing 'desperate tricks'

Yesterday DailyMail.com exclusively revealed the shocking allegation that Hunter Biden's lawyers played a 'desperate trick' to hide incriminating information from the judge in his criminal case.

Now we can show the bombshell emails and fiery exchanges between lawyers filed in the case – as the Delaware judge decides on Hunter's 'sweetheart' plea deal with prosecutors.

The controversy is the latest in a series of scandals and intrigue surrounding the legal team of the tax cheat First Son.

Tuesday's incident began when House Ways and Means Committee Chairman Jason Smith wrote to Hunter's judge Maryellen Noreika, suggesting that she toss his 'wrist slap' plea deal with prosecutors.

In his 'amicus' filing, the senior Republican lawmaker highlighted voluminous evidence that the Department of Justice obstructed the five-year investigation into the President's son.

Delaware Judge Maryellen Noreika was asked to toss Hunter's 'wrist slap' deal with prosecutors 

He included with the 448-pages of Congressional testimony and documents from two IRS whistleblowers who worked Hunter's case, claiming that Justice officials tipped off Hunter's lawyers to investigators' plans, let the statute of limitations run out on some of Hunter's alleged felonies, and told investigators not to look into potential crimes involving Joe Biden.

The testimony, backed up with emails and texts and published by Smith's committee last month, sent shockwaves through Washington DC and caused even Hunter's previous defenders in the media to question the no-prison deal he struck with Trump-appointed Delaware prosecutor David Weiss, deemed a mere 'slap on the wrist' by top Republicans.

So Hunter's lawyers were aghast when, on the eve of Hunter's first appearance in the Delaware criminal court, Smith served up the evidence to the judge in a searing brief that threatened to unpick his pact with the DoJ.

New filings in the case reveal what they did next – and shocking allegations of last-ditch skullduggery to prevent the evidence from being a public part of the case.

A lawyer at the firm Latham & Watkins told one of his staffers to call the court and get Smith's filing taken down.

Litigation services director Jessica Bengels phoned a Delaware court clerk on Tuesday morning, and minutes later Smith's bombshell filing was deleted from the docket.

Then Smith's lawyer, Theodore Kittila, wrote to the judge with a shocking allegation: Bengels had lied and pretended to be from his office, not Latham, in order to get the incriminating evidence on Hunter hidden.

'We promptly contacted the Clerk's office, and we were advised that someone contacted the Court representing that they worked with my office and that they were asking the Court to remove this from the docket,' Kittila wrote to the judge Tuesday afternoon. 'We immediately advised that this was inaccurate.'

House Ways and Means Committee Chairman Jason Smith filed a brief to Judge Noreika, suggesting that she toss Hunter's 'sweetheart' plea deal with Delaware prosecutors due to claims they gave the President's son preferential treatment

Emails between Clark's team and Kittila's team intensified. Yesterday, Hunter's Latham & Watkins lawyer Matthew Salerno filed a response claiming the incident was down to 'an unfortunate and unintentional miscommunication'

In a fiery email exchange with Kittila about the apparent skullduggery, Hunter's lawyer Chris Clark denied any improper conduct and claimed 'the clerk took the filing down on their own accord'.

'I stand by all of my statements and I hope you have an affidavit from the clerk in support of yours,' he wrote.

 Latham & Watkins litigation services director Jessica Bengels was accused of posing as Kittila's aide

But Kittila already had the clerk's account backing up his claims. He filed to the judge an email from Delaware court team lead Samantha Grimes accusing Latham staffer Bengels of posing as his aide.

'She said she worked with Theodore Kittila and it was important the document was removed immediately or they could file a motion to seal,' Grimes wrote in the email.

The judge issued an order giving Hunter's legal team until 9pm that day to explain themselves, or she would slap them with sanctions.

'It appears that the caller misrepresented her identity and who she worked for in an attempt to improperly convince the clerk's office to remove the amicus materials from the docket,' the order said.

Minutes ahead of the deadline, Hunter's Latham & Watkins lawyer Matthew Salerno filed a response claiming the incident was down to 'an unfortunate and unintentional miscommunication'.

'We have no idea how the misunderstanding occurred, but our understanding is there was no misrepresentation,' he wrote to Judge Noreika on Tuesday evening.

He claimed that Bengels, the Latham staffer accused of pretending to be from another law firm, has a phone number which should show up as 'LATHAM' on the clerk's caller ID.

Salerno attached an affidavit from Bengels, in which she claimed she initially called a court clerk called 'Julia', from her Latham phone, identified herself truthfully, but was later called back by a different clerk, Samantha.

'At around 11.54am, another court employee called my Latham & Watkins phone number (which I believe she knew through caller ID) to let me know she would be removing the material from the docket,' Bengels wrote. 'She did not ask which law firm I was affiliated with, and at no time during this call did I mention anything about my law firm affiliation.

'I believe there may have been some confusion when Julia passed the information on to the other court employee, resulting in a mistaken understanding that I had called from Mr. Kittila's firm.

'I am completely confident that I never indicated that I was calling from Mr. Kittila's firm or that I worked with him in any way.'

Judge Noreika has yet to give her opinion of Latham's explanation.

A top Republican's lawyer included in his filing a copy of an email from the Delaware clerk Sam Grimes, backing up his claims. 'The woman who called was a Jessica Bengels,' she wrote, adding Bengels' phone number

However, in her Tuesday order she also placed a temporary seal on Smith's filing, meaning it would not be public until after Hunter's plea hearing Wednesday.

Mike Howell, director of conservative think tank the Heritage Foundation's Oversight Project, told DailyMail.com that the latest twist in the courtroom saga was 'crazy' and the alleged actions of Hunter's lawyers 'reek of desperation'.

'Something crazy is happening right now in the Hunter Biden case,' he said. 'The judge ordered the sealing of transcripts that have been publicly available on the House website for over a month and which a House Committee voted to release in compliance with applicable rules.

'The courageous whistleblowers did the right thing in the right way – they provided sworn testimony protected under U.S. law.

'If anyone lied to the court, they need to be punished. Otherwise, we can only expect the same behavior in the future. It is unbelievable that we are dealing with these 11th-Hour procedural shenanigans that reek of desperation.'

Hunter's lead attorney Clark worked for Latham & Watkins but left to start his own firm, Clark Smith Villazor, in April.

It appears Hunter continued to use Latham, though company lawyer Salerno only filed notifying the judge of this on July 20, with his certificate of good standing appearing on the docket yesterday.

The IRS whistleblowers claim that while still at Latham, Clark told prosecutors that pursuing a case against Hunter would be 'career suicide' – an example of some of the alleged bullish tactics common at the firm.

Latham itself touted its 'fearsomeness' and 'ferocious litigators' in an October 2020 press release, and has attracted controversy for its no-holds-barred approach to legal services.

It has had a deep relationship with Russian state-owned companies that began in 1992 and includes VTB Bank and natural gas producer Novatek. Latham ended its Russia operation in March 2022 when Putin invaded Ukraine.

A 2020 report by Law Students for Climate Accountability gave the company an 'F' scorecard rating, saying Latham was among the five worst firms for 'both transactions and litigation exacerbating climate change', with $94.8 billion of work for the fossil fuel industry from 2015 to 2019.

Despite the Biden administration trumpeting green credentials, it has become a new home for several top ex-Latham lawyers – with some returning to the firm after their government work.

Latham partner Nicholas McQuaid had a 20-month stint as acting DoJ Criminal Division head before returning to the firm in November, and Andrew Strelka served eight months as White House Senior Tax Counsel in 2021 before rejoining his old Latham job.

Former Deputy White House Counsel Jonathan Su took his role between senior Latham roles, and Deputy Interior Secretary Tommy Beaudreau joined government after a Latham job.

Clients of the firm have included serial child abuser and former Donald Trump advisor George Nader; Lori Laughlin, the actress at the center of the college admissions scandal; and Republican Senator and alleged insider trader Richard Burr.
