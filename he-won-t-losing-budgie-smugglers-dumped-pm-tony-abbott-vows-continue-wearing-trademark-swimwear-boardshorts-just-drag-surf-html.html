Tony Abbott vows to continue wearing his trademark swimwear

He's done working in the highest office of the land but for former prime minister Tony Abbott, some things never change.

At the end of a lengthy interview with 2GB radio host Ray Hadley, Mr Abbott reflected on his future and pledged to jump back in the surf in his infamous budgie smugglers.

When the broadcaster pleaded with him to reconsider his trademark swimwear - 'not the budgie smugglers, wear some boardies' - the nation's one-time leader was defiant.

After discussing his mental state, the future of the Turnbull government and his legacy as leader, Mr Abbott said he was not sitting at home feeling sorry for himself. 

He has already been out volunteering with the Rural Fire Service - an act of giving back to the community he was often unable to make time for as the nation's commander-in-chief.

'I've never been someone who just wanted to hang around and smell the roses, that's never been my idea of a day well spent,' he said. 

'But I did get out and spend a day with my brigade the Davidson Rural Fire Brigade. 

'It was great to be with the boys. I haven't spent enough time with them.'

Mr Abbott said his wife, Margie, was 'coping OK' since he had been dismissed from the country's top job.

'She did everything that was asked of her with grace and dignity,' he said of his beloved partner.

'Margie's never been, I suppose, a political partisan, she's not an avid spouse if you like, but nevertheless she was a very supportive spouse throughout all the ups and downs of my public life.'

Now a backbench MP, Mr Abbott has said he will consider his future in Parliament early in the new year. He has remarked he is too young to retire. 

He finished the interview reflecting upon the honour of being elected the nation's leader.

'It is an extraordinary honour to be the Prime Minister of our country. 

'In all the years since Federation there's only been 29 and to be the 28th Prime Minister of this country - and we are such an extraordinary place...

'We really are an example to the wider world in everything, it seems, except this extraordinary and incomprehensible propensity to change our prime ministers like we change our clothes.'

He listed the government's achievements - including stopping the boats but warned of the nation's 'age of sour, bitter character assassination' - where the country has seen five prime ministers in five years
