Twitter bot to counter cyberbullying with positivism

“@AmazingCinta I have a robot cavity, because you are so sweet. #TheNiceBot”

“@usa2016election Calculating how incredible you are would require mathematical equations far more advanced than my programming. #TheNiceBot”

“@OURHONEYFROOT Supernovas wish they were as bright as your smile. #TheNiceBot”

Some even sound slightly ... more-than-friends.

“@Lili_140803 My processors overheat when I think about how great you are. #TheNiceBot”

“@ERlNxx Just thinking about you sends a positive surge through my circuits. #TheNiceBot”

And inevitably, there has been the rare misfire.

“@BillCosby You give me a happiness overload. #TheNiceBot”

(Then again, Cosby probably hasn't been getting too many positive messages lately, so maybe this was appreciated.)

The NiceBot was launched on October 1 with the mission to “make the world a nicer place, one tweet at a time”.

Alexandra Penn, founder of Champions Against Bullying, says the organisation, which works with children, parents and educators in the US and Canada, wanted to do something different to draw attention to online toxicity.

“Last year, we created a PSA that called attention to teenagers who had committed suicide from being bullied,” Penn says in a statement introducing the NiceBot. “This year, we wanted to combat the negativity of bullying with something more positive.”

At best, the NiceBot might be a helpful way to spread awareness of cyberbullying. At worst, it’s a harmless, feel-good gimmick – but it isn't likely to do much to alleviate hostile behaviour online, says Sameer Hinduja, co-director of the Cyberbullying Research Centre and a professor of criminology at Florida Atlantic University.

Nevertheless, he says, he commends Champions Against Bullying “at least a little bit for trying to do something novel, trying to do something that’s a little bit innovative to spread positivity and make kindness go viral”.

Cyberbullying is devastating, particularly for kids and teens, because it feels so personal, he says. An insult, or a compliment, doesn't carry as much weight if it comes across as generic or random – which makes a spambot seem like a questionable choice for an online agent of change.

“When you think of somebody who says something kind, it doesn't feel like spam because there’s a personal touch there – a person smiles, it’s warm,” Hinduja says.

“Unfortunately, I feel like everyone’s just going to dismiss this without giving it a second thought, particularly because the user name has the word ‘bot’ in it.”

David Weinberger, a senior researcher at the Berkman Centre for Internet and Society at Harvard University and an author who focuses on the impact of technology on ideas, apparently agrees:

“Thank you, @NiceBot! That means so little coming from a bot!”

But plenty of other people seem to appreciate it: It's “a nice way to start the day”, noted one pleased Twitter user. And even if the NiceBot can't possibly do much to make the digital landscape a safer or kinder place, it might inspire other, more effective or innovative ideas, says Hinduja.

“Instead of handing out a bracelet or a sticker, [Champions Against Bullying] is using programming to tap into social media networks in order to do something creative,” he says. “So I think that might be the take-home point. I don't know how much this is going to accomplish, but they tried, and there's something to be said about that.”

Despite an uphill battle, the NiceBot tweets on in the cynical cyberspace – a relentless digital cheerleader, trying again every 36 seconds.

“@Xzeynep_beyzaxx Thanks for just being you. #TheNiceBot”
