Who Was Rock Hudson? - Hollywood Star Rock Hudson Facts

If you aren’t already falling for the big, beautiful eyes and sturdy jawline of Jake Picking as Rock Hudson in Netflix’s new alternate-history series, Hollywood, let us break it to you: You will be. Picking plays the real-life legendary actor, but with an important twist: He’s living in an alternate-history Tinseltown where race, gender, and sexual orientation didn't remain the barriers they were in reality.

Hollywood pulls important details from Hudson’s true story to build out its dream world, but his real life was every bit as fascinating—and, at times, far more heart-wrenching. To understand the decisions Hollywood makes, particularly around the more intimate details of Hudson’s tale, you’ll need to know a bit more about the man himself. Here’s who the real Rock Hudson was and became.

Rock Hudson wasn’t his real name.

Like Marilyn Monroe, Hudson adopted a flashier name when he began his acting career. He was born Roy Harold Scherer Jr. in 1925, though his legal name became Roy Fitzgerald when his stepfather, Wallace Fitzgerald, adopted him without his consent, according to The Scribner Encyclopedia of American Lives.

Bettmann//Getty Images

Rock Hudson was born in Winnetka, Illinois.

A former truck driver from Illinois, he briefly joined the U.S. Navy between 1944 and 1946, then moved to Los Angeles and donned the new title Rock Hudson in 1947, when his agent Henry Willson suggested it. The “Rock” comes from Rock of Gibraltar, and “Hudson” is representative of the Hudson River.

He had no acting training, but he made producers pay attention.

A pretty face will get you far in La La Land, then and now, and Hudson knew it. He was well aware of his chiseled good looks, despite his lack of acting training, so he would pose outside of studio gates to try and catch the attention of someone influential, or he’d send photographs of himself to producers.

For his biography of Hudson, All That Heaven Allows, writer Mark Griffin interviewed Ken Maley, a friend of Hudson’s. “Just think about the young Rock Hudson—this big stud standing up against his truck outside the studio gates,” Maley said. “He’d roll up his sleeves and just stand there, like some giant ad for sex, waiting to be noticed day after day.”

Eventually, at a local soiree, Hudson connected with Willson, and the influential agent signed him on.

Michael Ochs Archives//Getty Images

Rock Hudson quickly became one of Hollywood’s most beloved bachelors, due in large part to his looks.

By the end of the 1950s, he was as big as Brad Pitt.

Despite needing somewhere around 30 takes to say one line in his embarrassing first screen test—yes, that really did happen, according to Hollywood co-creator Ryan Murphy—Hudson eventually became one of the most popular and acclaimed actors in the game. Hudson acted in his first film at about the age of 23; he was nearly 60 when he died.

In 1948, he clinched a contract with Universal Pictures, which provided him with acting lessons, but his real fame skyrocketed in the 1950s. He starred in the 1956 film Giant alongside Elizabeth Taylor and James Dean, earning him an Oscar nomination, and in 1959 he wooed Doris Day in Pillow Talk. By the time of his death, he’d been in more than 60 films.

Vittoriano Rastelli//Getty Images

Hudson became a fixture of Hollywood stardom during the 1950s and 1960s, pictured here in 1967.

He was a closeted gay man, groomed by his stepfather and agent to be less “effeminate.”

Despite his overwhelming efforts to appear straight, it became a (somewhat poorly kept) secret that Hudson was gay. He had multiple lovers in Hollywood, but long before he became an acting legend, the abusive men in his life conditioned him to behave unlike himself.

In an interview on NPR’s Fresh Air, Griffin told Terry Gross, “Sadly, Rock did have this abusive, alcoholic stepfather, Wallace Fitzgerald, who was a former Marine. And Rock tells this sort of tragic story that when he built up the courage to tell his stepfather that he had these ambitions to be an actor, his stepfather hit him—and not only that but I think confiscated any toys that he thought were in some way effeminate and discouraged any theatrical ambitions that Rock may have had.”

Frank Worth, Courtesy of Capital Art//Getty Images

Hudson was trained by his agent to become the stereotypical Hollywood macho man.

Hollywood’s uncomfortable depictions of sex between Hudson and his agent are drawn from reality.

All That Heaven Allows explained that Willson had far from a sparkling reputation. He was called “disgusting,” “diabolical,” and “predatory.” Played by Jim Parsons in Hollywood, Willson grew up with a father who was vice president of the Columbia Phonograph Company; unlike Hudson, he was no stranger to fame.

But he and Hudson were not so different in other ways—both young men were gay, and both were bullied by their fathers for their “feminine” behaviors. Willson’s father sent his son to an all-male boarding school in an attempt to make him manlier, which Griffin wrote may have contributed to Willson’s uncanny talent for sculpting macho men out of his clients.

Netflix

In Hollywood, Jim Parsons plays Henry Willson, Rock Hudson’s predatory agent.

Willson also had a knack for plucking attractive would-be stars out of obscurity, and Hudson was just another perfect pick. He trained Hudson to no longer employ his “fluttery giggle” or “girlish curl to his upper lip,” Griffin wrote.

Yet Willson preyed upon Hudson’s homosexuality himself. Almost immediately, people began to suspect their relationship went beyond the professional. MGM Studios’ head of talent at the time, Lucille Ryman Carroll, told Griffin that Willson would sometimes hold Hudson’s hand. In the NPR interview with Gross, Griffin said, “It was fairly well-known that if you were a Henry Willson client, as Tony Curtis once expressed it, you probably had to sexually express yourself to Henry.”

Hudson was married to Phyllis Gates for three years. It might have been an arranged marriage.

Life magazine published a profile calling Hudson “Hollywood’s Most Handsome Bachelor” in 1955, Griffin wrote. The title was flattering but not altogether good for Hudson’s image as the quintessential man’s man: The first line of the Life story read, “Fans are urging 29-year-old Hudson to get married—or explain why not.”

After years of claiming he was “marriage shy,” Hudson married clean-cut beauty Phyllis Gates. Arguments still abound over whether it was an arranged sham, but the evidence points to Willson orchestrating the ordeal. According to an old Guardian obituary of Gates, she was a farm girl from Minnesota who landed a job working for Willson in Los Angeles. When pressure mounted for Hudson to tie the knot, Willson “contrived for Gates and Hudson to date each other after they had met in his office.” Hudson proposed to her in that same office; he and Gates were married a month later, and Willson immediately leaked the news to the press.

Pictorial Parade//Getty Images

Rock Hudson and Phyllis Gates married in 1955. Their marriage lasted three years.

But the marriage was not a particularly happy one, and the couple divorced three years later.

Gates wrote about the relationship in her book, My Husband, Rock Hudson, claiming that she was duped into the romance and that Hudson was an abusive partner. This is very possible, though Griffin told NPR, “A lot of people that I interviewed find it very surprising that Phyllis wouldn't have had some sort of clue about Rock's sexual proclivities, as she was working for Henry Willson, top powerbroker in the industry. And if extras and bit players knew the score about Rock Hudson, why wouldn't she, being Henry Willson's secretary?”

His death was one of the most significant moments of the AIDS crisis during the 1980s.

Hudson’s eventual death from AIDS in 1985 served as a turning point in the crisis. All That Heaven Allows stated that Hudson’s physician, Michael Gottlieb, “would describe Rock as ‘the single most influential AIDS patient ever.’”

In his 1987 book And the Band Played On: Politics, People, and the AIDS Epidemic, journalist Randy Shilts, who would later die of AIDS himself, wrote, “There was AIDS before Rock Hudson and AIDS after.”

LGI Stock//Getty Images

Rock Hudson died from AIDS complications in 1985.

None of these statements hyperbolize the magnitude of Hudson’s public confession. His battle with AIDS eventually led him to make a life-changing decision: He would reveal his diagnosis to the world—and by proxy, his implied homosexuality. Griffin described that in June 1984, Hudson went to a dermatologist to have a blemish on his neck checked out. It turned out to be Kaposi sarcoma, a cancer frequently found in AIDS patients, and the actor cried for a week. When he eventually died, it was the first high-profile celebrity death openly associated with the disease.

Said Joan Rivers in a 1985 People magazine story, published shortly before Hudson’s death, “Two years ago, when I hosted a benefit for AIDS, I couldn’t get one major star to turn out. … Rock’s admission is a horrendous way to bring AIDS to the attention of the American public, but by doing so, Rock, in his life, has helped millions in the process. What Rock has done takes true courage."
